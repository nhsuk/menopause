Symptoms - Menopause

Contents

  1. Overview
  2. Symptoms
  3. Treatment

It is likely you will have some menopausal symptoms. How long the symptoms last and how severe they are varies between people.

Symptoms usually start a few months or years before your periods stop, known as the perimenopause. They then usually continue for around 7 years from your last period.

Symptoms can change with time. Hot flushes and night sweats may improve and then you may develop low mood and anxiety.

Your symptoms may be worse if your menopause comes on suddenly, for example, as a result of surgery to remove your ovaries.

Changes to your periods

The first sign of the perimenopause is usually a change in the normal pattern of your periods.

You may start having either unusually light or heavy periods.

The frequency of your periods may also be affected. You may have them every 2 or 3 weeks, or you may not have them for months at a time.

Eventually, you'll stop having periods altogether. A year after your last period is the menopause.

Common menopausal symptoms

It is likely you will have menopausal symptoms for some time before and after your periods stop.

These can have a significant impact on your daily life.

Common symptoms include:

  • hot flushes – short, sudden feelings of heat or cold, usually in your face, neck and chest, which can make you sweaty
  • night sweats
  • difficulty sleeping – this may be a result of the night sweats and make you feel tired and irritable during the day
  • changes to your mood – this may be low mood, anxiety, mood swings and low self-esteem
  • problems with memory and concentration
  • palpitations – heartbeats that suddenly become more noticeable
  • headaches
  • muscle aches and joint pains
  • changing body shape and weight gain
  • skin changes including dry and itchy skin
  • a reduced sex drive (libido)
  • vaginal dryness and pain, itching or discomfort during sex
  • reduced muscle mass
  • recurrent urinary tract infections (UTIs)

The menopause can also increase your risk of developing other problems, such as osteoporosis, heart disease and type 2 diabetes.

Non-urgent advice: See a GP if:

  • you are having menopausal symptoms

They can offer advice and treatment to improve your symptoms and future health.
