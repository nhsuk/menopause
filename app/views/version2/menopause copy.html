Overview - Menopause

Contents

  1. Overview
  2. Symptoms
  3. Treatment

The menopause is when your ovaries stop releasing an egg every month and your periods end. Some of your hormone levels reduce and stay this way for the rest of your life.

Periods usually start to become less frequent over a few months or years before they stop altogether. Sometimes they can stop suddenly.

The menopause usually happens between the ages of 45 and 55.

Sometimes, menopause happens before 45 (early menopause).

Symptoms of the menopause

Menopausal symptoms can last for many years, sometimes up to 10. This period of time is called the perimenopause.

Symptoms include:

  • hot flushes
  • night sweats
  • difficulty sleeping
  • low mood, mood swings or anxiety
  • vaginal dryness
  • reduced sex drive (libido)
  • problems with memory and concentration

Menopausal symptoms can be severe and have a significant impact on your life. It is important to get support to keep you well.

Non-urgent advice: See a GP if:

  • you are having menopausal symptoms

They will offer you more support and answer any questions you may have. Usually, you will be diagnosed based on your symptoms.

Treatments for menopausal symptoms

The main treatments are:

  • hormone replacement therapy (HRT) – tablets, skin patches, gels and implants that relieve menopausal symptoms by replacing oestrogen
  • vaginal oestrogen creams, lubricants or moisturisers for vaginal dryness
  • cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) – a type of talking therapy that can help with low mood and anxiety

Eating a healthy, balanced diet and exercising regularly can also improve some menopausal symptoms.

Your GP may refer you to a menopause specialist if your symptoms do not improve after trying treatment or if you're unable to take HRT.

What causes the menopause?

The menopause is caused by a reduction in the level of your body's sex hormones, which happens as you get older.

The menopause can occur at an early age for many other reasons.

Causes of the menopause include:

  • surgery to remove the ovaries (oophorectomy)
  • some cancer treatments such as chemotherapy or radiotherapy
  • having an underlying condition, such as as Down's syndrome or Addison's disease
