Early menopause

Early menopause is when your periods stop before the age of 40. It’s also called premature ovarian insufficiency.

An early menopause can happen naturally, or be caused by certain conditions or treatments, such as cancer treatment or surgery to remove your ovaries.

Symptoms of early menopause

Symptoms of early menopause are the same as for menopause. The main symptom is usually a change to your periods, for example they happen less often.

You may also have:

  • hot flushes, when you have short, sudden feelings of heat or cold in your face, neck or chest that can make you feel sweaty and dizzy
  • difficulty sleeping
  • anxiety, low mood or mood swings
  • problems with memory and concentration (brain fog)
  • vaginal dryness and pain, itching or discomfort during sex
  • reduced sex drive (libido)

Read more about the symptoms of menopause

Non-urgent advice: See a GP if:

  • you are under 40 and you have menopause symptoms

Having early menopause can increase your risk of:

  • weak bones that break more easily (osteoporosis)
  • conditions that affect the heart and blood vessels (cardiovascular disease)

A GP can prescribe treatments that reduces these risks.


Diagnosing early menopause

The GP may be able to diagnose early menopause based on your symptoms and medical history.

They may ask if a close relative such as your mother, sister or aunt had early menopause as the condition can run in families.

You may have blood tests to measure your body's hormone levels.

In some cases you may be referred to specialist for further testing, to find out what’s causing early menopause. For example, you may be offered an ultrasound scan to check your ovaries.


Treating early menopause

Both the symptoms and the health risks of early menopause are caused by a drop in hormone levels in your body.

Medicines can help top-up your hormone levels which will both help treat symptoms and prevent complications.

Medicines you may be offered include:

  • hormone replacement therapy (HRT)
  • the combined contraceptive pill - this is used for its hormonal effects, and not as a contraceptive

They cannot stop early menopause.

You may need to take the combined pill or HRT until you’re 51 years old. This is the age when menopause usually happens naturally.

If you have had certain types of cancer, such as hormone-sensitive breast cancer, you may not be able to have HRT or the combined pill.


Lifestyle changes

Eating well, exercising and looking after your mental wellbeing can help with symptoms during menopause. It can also help you keep as well as possible in the future.

Do

  • if possible, get plenty of rest
  • try to go to sleep and get up in the morning at the same time every day as this should improve the quality of your sleep
  • eat a healthy diet
  • have calcium-rich food like cheese, yoghurt, kale and okra to help keep bones healthy
  • exercise regularly, try including activities you do on your feet, like walking, running or dancing
  • try relaxing activities like yoga, tai chi or meditation
  • talk to other people going through the same thing
  • talk to a doctor before taking herbal supplements or complementary medicines
  • take a regular vitamin D supplement as vitamin D helps keep bones healthy

Don't

  • do not smoke
  • do not drink more than the recommended alcohol limit

Both smoking and drinking too much alcohol increases the risk of cardiovascular disease and osteoporosis.


Fertility

Early menopause will make it more difficult to get pregnant.

Your GP or specialist can discuss this with you, and the options available to you.

If you’re going to have treatment that might cause early menopause, for example some cancer treatments, you may be able to store your eggs for later use.


Causes of early menopause

Early menopause is caused by a drop in levels of certain hormones, particularly the hormone oestrogen.

Early menopause can happen naturally, or be caused by certain conditions or treatments, such as cancer treatment or surgery to remove your ovaries.

If your mother, sister or aunt had early menopause then there is a greater risk that you may also have one.

Sometimes early menopause is caused by conditions including:

  • genetic issues, such as Turner syndrome
  • autoimmue disease, where the immune system affects your body
  • some infections, such as tuberculosis, malaria and mumps, but this is very rare

If your menopause is caused by a condition, then treating the condition may stop your early menopause. Your GP or specialist can discuss this with you.


Getting support

Early menopause can be difficult and upsetting.

Talking therapies, such as counselling, and support groups may be helpful.

Find out about NHS talking therapies.

Online support groups and charities include:

  • The Daisy Network – a support group for people affected by early menopause
  • healthtalk.org – provides information about early menopause, including women talking about their own experiences
  • Fertility friends - a support network for people with fertility problems
  • Macmillian Cancer Support - offers a range of resources for people affected both by cancer and cancer treatments
